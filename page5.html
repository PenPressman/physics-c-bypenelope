Intro to Simple Harmonic Motion

It will be easier to learn all of this through examples, but before we start: simple harmonic motion (SHM) refers to periodic motion due to a consistent restoring force. In an SHM scenario, we‚Äôll have an equilibrium point in the middle of the period (the time it takes to complete a cycle). Think of the period as the time one oscillation takes; meaning, frequency will give you a value in a new unit: hz, for how many oscillations the system experiences in an amount of time. I‚Äôll go into more detail about how the rest of the formulas work in each example, but for me it has been helpful to think of things broadly first. Also, in this unit there will often be multiple viable ways to solve things given the many relationships between variables, so figure out what comes easiest to you, or what makes the most sense (though hopefully, eventually, everything will!).

If you want a good intro to this unit, play around with a string and different masses for a bit.

Ok, the main part: the reason we have the relationships between 2œÄ, œâ, f and T (which you can see on the formula page), is that you can think of a mass on a spring (or any simple harmonic motion, for that matter) as an imaginary particle moving in a circle, except in reality, the circle is just a back-and-forth path. g-fields

Quickly, remember that the g-fields we‚Äôre talking about refer to a situation in which a mass/object feels a force and thus experiences acceleration due to gravity, caused by a large mass it is near (ususally earth/another planet).

The first thing I recommenddoing is separating your view of g-fields into those outside planets and those inside. Before you even start thinking about the formulas, this will help you remember not to just find a way to plug in a fraction of r or anything like that for the radial value. We‚Äôre already familiar with the outside part, which you have already seen in the form of g=-9.8 at/near the surface of earth. To derive a simple formula, we can use what we know about Fg, in that it is equal to both mg and a times the general g formula (see formula page), then cancel m to find a value for g. For the inside version, we can use density to solve for g in terms of the radius the second mass is at. Using the relationship between density, mass, and volume, we can get the mass of the larger object (earth/a planet) in terms of the changing radius, and then plug that back into our general g equation and cancel repetitive variables to find gins.

Once we know all this, we can connect back to SHM by noting that whenever you have a force equaling a constant times a changing distance variable (‚Äúr‚Äù in this case), things start to look really similar to fs=kx, and we can employ the tools of SHM! Try practicing finding ‚ÄúKgrav‚Äù aka the gravitational constant, and using that to find the period of a theoretical basketball being dropped‚Ä¶ through the earth. Given that we now it‚Äôs harmonic, and the fact that we can use the formulas for g-ins and g-outs to see how the force will change as the basketball drops, we‚Äôll be able to conclude that, given a starting drop, the ball would fall through the center of the earth, pause at the other end and turn around, and repeat back and forth forever. Yes, this relies on the assumption we could remove all other variables and drill a basketball-sized hole in the entirety of the earth, but still, it‚Äôs fun.

Escape Velocity

Now that we now how hard objects get pulled into earth, we need a way to calculate how fast they need to move to break free of that force (*that explanation is not at all in technical terms but that‚Äôs how I think of it*). This is called: escape velocity!

We know that since we want to escape the pull, we need to reach where Ug = 0. And, since we want to find the threshold for this, not just some speed that is more than enough, we want K to equal 0 as well. This means we‚Äôll use up allll our energy breaking free, and will be successful with no extra energy to spare. That was repetitive because once you know this, the math part is easy to solve. Kepler‚Äôs Laws

Kepler‚Äôs laws are experimentally proven rules that help us understand how planets move.
There are three laws, memorize them and their implications:

1

All planets move in elliptical orbits with the sun at one focus. (And objects orbit planets in the same way.)If you need a reminder of what an ellipse looks like, look at the image at the top of this page: note, the speed of the moving sphere is inaccurate for simplification, and after reading these laws you‚Äôll know why!


2

Planets sweep across equal area in equal time.
When a planet is closer to a focus near the side of an ellipse, since there is less distance between the planet and the focus, we know it will move faster. When the planet is moving on the other side, farther from the focus, it will be moving slower but have more area covered given the increased distance. Overall, there will be even area swept over in even time. This also confirmed conservation of angular momentum, since we know that change in angular position over change in time is equal to œâ, and thus Iœâ will be constant (L=Iœâ).


3

The third law gives us the formulas that relate orbital period to orbital radius (see formula sheet). Importantly, there are two formulas for two kinds of orbits: elliptical and circular. Remember that for elliptical orbits, the orbital radius gets swapped for half the value of the semi-major axis (aka the length of the ellipse at its farthest points). Speaking of orbits‚Ä¶
