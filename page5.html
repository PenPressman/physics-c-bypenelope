General SHM

Hallmark of SHM: d2x/dt2= -ω2x

x=Acos(ωt+Φ) (where “A” is amplitude)
v=-Aωsin(ωt+Φ)
a=-Aω2cos(ωt+Φ)

xmax=A
vmax=Aω
amax=Aω2


Periods and frequence: T=1/f, ω=2πf

**remember to review how to find the combined spring constant of a system with multiple springs, when they are either in series or parallel!

Mass-Springs

d2x/dt2= -k/mx
Tmass-spring = 2π√(m/k) “two pi with a squirt of milk”

*the following can be found by using the relationships between T, ω, and f:

ωmass-spring = √(k/m)
fmass-spring = 1/(2π) √(k/m)

Pendulums

Simple Pendulums

d2θ/dt2= -g/Lθ (where “L” is the length of the pendulum)
Tsimple-pendulum = 2π√(L/g) “two pi with a squirt of leg”

*the following can be found by using the relationships between T, ω, and f:

ωsimple-pendulum = √(g/L)
fsimple-pendulum = 1/(2π) √(g/L)

Physical Pendulums

d2θ/dt2= -mgD/Iθ (where “I” is the moment of inertia of the pendulum system and D is the distance between the COM and axis of rotation)
Tphysical-pendulum = 2π√(I/mgD) “two pi with a squirt of I- my god!”

*the following can be found by using the relationships between T, ω, and f:

ωphysical-pendulum = √(mgD/I)
fphysical-pendulum = 1/(2π) √(mgD/I)

Torsion Pendulums

Τ = -Kθ (where “K” is the torsion constant, kappa and “τ” is the restoring torque of the pendulum)
Ttorsion-pendulum = 2π√(I/K) “two pi with a squirt of ick”
ftorsion-pendulum = 1/(2π) √(K/I)

Gravity

g-fields

ggeneral= GmM/r2 (where “G” is the gravitational constant, “M” is the mass of Earth, “m” is the mass of the other object and “r” is the distance between earth’s COM and the object’s COM.)

gat surface of Earth= GMearth/Rearth2

ginside Earth= GMearth/Rearth3 r (where “r” is the distance from the earth’s COM)

Gravitational Potential Energy (new unit perspective)

First, review past Ug formulas!

Ug = -GmM/r

Orbits

escape velocity: vE = √2GM/r

circular orbits: vC = √GM/r
kinetic energy in a circular orbit: K = GmM/2r

Kepler’s Laws

Periods of circular orbits: T2 = 4π2/GM r3

Periods of eliptical orbits: T2 = 4π2/GM a (where “a” is half the length of the ellipse)3
