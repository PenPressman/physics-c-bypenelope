I’ve structured these thermodynamics pages differently than the mech E & M ones for a few reasons. First, because there’s no AP test, I’m not including random concepts that you may or may not need to know, since every course will be different; instead, I’m focusing more on understanding. Second, “formulas” don’t really work the same way. There are no more shortcuts. Well, there are some, but learning them is more effort than just understanding the concepts behind them AND we’re too far along now to get by via just memorizing stuff. As Resnick said in one of my favorite textbooks:

”The easiest way to master this subject is to follow the reasoning I give you. That way, you don’t have to store too many things in your head. Early on, when there are four or five formulas, you can memorize all of them and you can try every one of them until something works, but, after a couple of weeks, you will have hundreds of formulas, and you cannot memorize all of them. You cannot resort to trial and error. You have to know the logic.”

Trust him, he knows more than we do.

The other thing to know about this unit is that we often have multiple proofs of the same concept. That’s because (hopefully) the laws of physics always hold true, otherwise they wouldn’t be laws. This may sound weird to say, but you’ll soon see what I mean (get ready for entropy!). Alright, let’s start.

Units and Constants for Intro to Thermodyanmics

Remember, if there’s not much new, review what you can already do. Also, for units with multiple options, I included them in order of how often they are used in practice problems (following the standard ones).

Units

temperature (T) kelvin [K] = 1° celcius [C] - 273.15° C
pressure (P) pascal [Pa] = 9.87 atmospheres [atm] = 1 newton per square meter [N/m2]
density (ρ) kilograms per cubic meter [kg/m3]
specific heat capacity (C) joule per kilogram per kelvin (J/kg/K)
specific heat (c) calories [c]
heat (Q) joules [J]
