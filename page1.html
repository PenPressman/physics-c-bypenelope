Kinematics

Back to basics! Kinematics are pretty fundamental, and you'll need them a lot later on. Aside from memorizing the kinematic equations, go over the principle concepts and make sure you understand everything, especially common topics like free-fall, projectile motion, relative velocity and more. Since this is the first unit, make sure to review calculus skills (specifically deriving and integrating functions) and calculator skills (nsolve is the best :).

The Kinematic Equations

v = v0 + a*t

v0 = v02 + 2*a*(Δx)

(Δx) = v0*t + ½*a*t2

(Δx) = ½(v + v0)*t

v* = Velocity (which you can also think of as final velocity during the time “t” you are using)
v0* = Initial velocity
t = Time
x* = Displacement in one direction (I use "x" as my go-to variable, but make sure to keep in mind what it is actually representing in a given scenario)
a* = Acceleration (must be constant in order to apply kinematics! Remember that ay due to gravity is equal to -9.8m/s, but you can round to -10 on the AP)

*Note, aside from time, all variables must be analyzed using individual components (ie. x, y, or z direction).

Relative Velocity

VPS = VPW + VWS

(where each variable represents a reference frame, ie. Vperson to shore = Vperson to water + Vwater to shore)

You can learn more about the concept of relative velocity if you click the other button, but for now- to remember the formula, I like to think of it as splitting the two variables and adding a common one in the middle. In a nutshell, pick a variable and use it to split them!

Other Formulas

Speed = distance/t
Constant velocity: v = Δx/t
Average velocity: vavg = Δx/t
Constant acceleration: a = Δv/t
Average acceleration: aavg = Δv/t

For vectors, just remember the Pythagorean theorem: A2 = B2 + C2

Calculus Relationships

v = dx/dt
a = dv/dt = d2x/dx2
x = ∫v dt
v = ∫a dt
AUC (area under the curve) of a a/t graph is Δv
AUC (area under the curve) of a v/t graph is Δx

*Remember, to find Δx or Δv, make the integral definite and include the initial condition! Also, keep in mind components just like with the kinematic equations, since v and a are vectors (more explanation in the concept section).

Relative Velocity

I want to start with this because I think it's the most isolated concept from the rest of this unit, and once you get it… you get it. Basically, think of walking on one of those flat escalators or moving floor things at airports, whatever you want to call them. In terms of how long it takes you to move from one place in the airport to another, we can just measure the time it takes for you to move, say, 20 meters. So, let’s say that took 20 seconds, aka you are moving at 1m/s. When we think of it more specifically, that’s really how fast you are moving relative to the still ground of the airport. The measurement of how long it took you from here to there doesn’t tell you how fast you were walking, or how fast the flat escalator was moving. In order to determine either of those velocities, we need to compare all three frames to each other: you to the ground, you to the escalator, and the escalator to the ground. The formula for relative velocity analysis (click the formula button to see it) makes a lot of sense when you think about it – my speed in the airport is a combination of how fast I'm walking and how fast the escalator is moving me, hence the addition. The only thing to keep in mind, and the reason you should do some practice problems, is that these can get just a bit more complicated because you need to make sure to take components into account. Remember to calculate the velocities in the x and y directions separately. Then you’ll be good to go!

Vectors and Scalars

Speaking of splitting up components, this is important in general for any vector quantities . Because vectors take into account direction in addition to magnitude, you need to remember to split things into x and y. Stuff like mass, electric potential, and energy are scalars, so having a negative sign before an amount of energy would probably mean there was a change in energy or something like that. For vectors, like velocity, a negative sign refers to the direction of the velocity. A quick trick to remember is that if the velocity and acceleration have the same sign, the object is speeding up. If they are opposites, it is slowing down. That's why having "negative acceleration" does NOT necesarily entail slowing down (which might be confusing to those thinking of acceleration colloquially). If you're given individual components for something like velocity (ex. v=(2x)i + (4x 2 )j m/s), and you're given a scenario in which to find acceleration or some other variable, its probably easiest to keep everythinng in terms of individual components (this is called "unit vector notation"). However, if you need to find the magnitude of the vector, make sure to use the Pythagorean theorum (you can find it under the equations button), and then use SOCATOAH to find the angle. There will be a lot more to go over about vectors and scalars in later units, but for now, this is all you need. Oh and, if you want to add vectors in a different way (and for some reason you are given an example where this is easier), you can use the tip-to-tail method: draw the vectors by connecting each "tip" to the next one's "tail" and then find the final result. Remember, if one of them is negative, invert it before you add it (aka subtracting the vector).
