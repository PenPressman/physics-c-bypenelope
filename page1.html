Relative Velocity

I want to start with this because I think it's the most isolated concept from the rest of this unit, and once you get it‚Ä¶ you get it. Basically, think of walking on one of those flat escalators or moving floor things at airports, whatever you want to call them. In terms of how long it takes you to move from one place in the airport to another, we can just measure the time it takes for you to move, say, 20 meters. So, let‚Äôs say that took 20 seconds, aka you are moving at 1m/s. When we think of it more specifically, that‚Äôs really how fast you are moving relative to the still ground of the airport. The measurement of how long it took you from here to there doesn‚Äôt tell you how fast you were walking, or how fast the flat escalator was moving. In order to determine either of those velocities, we need to compare all three frames to each other: you to the ground, you to the escalator, and the escalator to the ground. The formula for relative velocity analysis (click the formula button to see it) makes a lot of sense when you think about it ‚Äì my speed in the airport is a combination of how fast I'm walking and how fast the escalator is moving me, hence the addition. The only thing to keep in mind, and the reason you should do some practice problems, is that these can get just a bit more complicated because you need to make sure to take components into account. Remember to calculate the velocities in the x and y directions separately. Then you‚Äôll be good to go!

Vectors and Scalars

Speaking of splitting up components, this is important in general for any vector quantities . Because vectors take into account direction in addition to magnitude, you need to remember to split things into x and y. Stuff like mass, electric potential, and energy are scalars, so having a negative sign before an amount of energy would probably mean there was a change in energy or something like that. For vectors, like velocity, a negative sign refers to the direction of the velocity. A quick trick to remember is that if the velocity and acceleration have the same sign, the object is speeding up. If they are opposites, it is slowing down. That's why having "negative acceleration" does NOT necesarily entail slowing down (which might be confusing to those thinking of acceleration colloquially). If you're given individual components for something like velocity (ex. v=(2x)i + (4x 2 )j m/s), and you're given a scenario in which to find acceleration or some other variable, it's probably easiest to keep everythinng in terms of individual components (this is called "unit vector notation"). However, if you need to find the magnitude of the vector, make sure to use the Pythagorean theorum (you can find it under the equations button), and then use SOCATOAH to find the angle. There will be a lot more to go over about vectors and scalars in later units, but for now, this is all you need. Oh and, if you want to add vectors in a different way (and for some reason you are given an example where this is easier), you can use the tip-to-tail method: draw the vectors by connecting each "tip" to the next one's "tail" and then find the final result. Remember, if one of them is negative, invert it before you add it (aka subtracting the vector).

In short, vectors = the dreaded (or maybe fun for some people?) return of geometry.

Projectile Motion

I‚Äôm not going to spend a lot of time on this because the main thing is just to do some practice problems, but here are a few notes to keep in mind.

1. Remember that acceleration due to gravity (aka ‚Äúg‚Äù) is in the y direction only !

2. Remember that you can find the max height by finding where v y is 0, since that is where the velocity will change direction and the projectile will start heading back down.

3. Unit 2 will explain this more, but aside from gravity, since there is no force acting on a projectile, it must have no other acceleration besides g. The exception to this would be if you had a problem where the projectile also had some sort of engine causing constant acceleration I guess.

4. Since the kinematic equations are consistently effective at solving for your missing variables, start by writing down everything you know (i.e. make a chart of x and y variables) and then go from there!
