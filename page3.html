Center of Mass

(As per usual, I’m starting with what I think of as the most isolated concept of this unit.)

You know how up until now, when we talk about the motion of random objects like cars or people or blocks, everything of the same mass kind of acts the same regardless of its shape? Well, it's easier to solve problems when we simplify that way, but in the real world objects are more complex. Objects have weird angles, different shapes, and, ready for it? Centers of mass! As the name suggests, the center of mass is the point at which all surrounding mass is equally distributed. When we think of things like gravity acting on an object, we actually calculate how it's acting on the objects center of mass. So, here’s what you need to know:

1. We can calculate the center of mass of a system algebraically or using calculus based on what we know about the object/its composition. For these calculations, see the formula sheet.
2. Always calculate the components of an object COM separately (x, y, z).
3. Sometimes we get lucky, and can use symmetry to find COM! For example, you know where the COM of a rectangle is (the midpoint vertically and horizontally).
4. For AP purposes, make sure to memorize derivations for common COM scenarios (ie rods, circles, triangles).

Energy

TLDR: the energy on the “before” side of a conservation of energy (COE) equation ALWAYS needs to be the same as the energy “after.” Did potential energy decrease? Well then, something else must have decreased.

If you don’t overthink it, conservation of energy is pretty simple, there are just a few kinds of energy we have to deal with (and the formulas are on the formula page):

The Types of Energy


1. Kinetic, the energy of movement (all moving objects have some).
2. Potential, either from gravity or something like a spring (I think of this as energy waiting to be turned kinetic, like when you almost drop a ball and it was juuust about to fall through the air if you let go).
3. What I call “loseable energy,” aka “Q,” from stuff like friction.

Solving COE Problems

I have exciting news, you now have a new option for solving a bunch of problems that previously required kinematics! This is another “need to do practice problems” situation, but basically just follow these steps:

1. Add up the energy a system had “before” and put that on the left side of an equation.
2. Add up all the energy a system has “after” and put that on the right side of the equation (so, equal to the left side).
3. Solve for missing pieces.

Collisions (Energy Perspective)


There are two types of collisions: elastic and inelastic. Elastic means that kinetic energy is conserved (so basically the objects bounce back). Inelastic means the objects lose kinetic energy, and fully inelastic means they stick together (think pieces of clay hitting each other and then continuing to move but now as one big glob). While you could figure problems out without memorizing the formula, I recommend memorizing the one I noted for elastic collisions.

One last note, the “height” in your gravitational potential calculation is not absolute, you just need to make sure that if gravitational potential changes, you are being accurate about the change in “h.” So, if a ball moves from 20m in the air to 10m, you could set 20m as 10m and 10 as 0m and still get everything right.

Work and Power

To be honest, my strategy to learn this was to memorize the formulas and then make sure I know what each term meant. But i’ll say this: be careful with your relationships between variables, don’t derive something instead of integrating! Other than that, just keep in mind these definitions:

Work is the transfer of energy by a force, and it is a SCALAR. (so, positive if it's transferring energy in vs negative if out, so the sign has NOTHING TO DO WITH DIRECTION. Keep in mind, for work to be done by a force, the force must be parallel to the resulting displacement. Work is also equal to the change in K (formula on formula page).
Power is the rate of work done with respect to time, and it is a SCALAR. Oh and remember the rate part, because it’ll help you with units (one J is equal to 1 Ns).

The main thing here is remembering that since these quantities are scalar, “how much work” a force does has no involvement with direction (other than the parallel thing). So, if I push a box forwards 10m and then backwards 10m, Fapplied by me is the same as if I push the box 20m forwards.

Momentum and Impulse

Same deal as energy, set up the conservation of momentum (COP) statement and plug stuff in. Basically, when you add up the value of the multiplied speeds and masses before a collision, the total should be the same after the collision.
