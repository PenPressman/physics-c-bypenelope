Center of Mass

(As per usual, I’m starting with what I think of as the most isolated concept of this unit.)
You know how up until now, when we talk about the motion of random objects like cars or people or blocks, everything of the same mass kind of acts the same regardless of its shape? Well, it's easier to solve problems when we simplify that way, but in the real world objects are more complex. Objects have weird angles, different shapes, and, ready for it? Centers of mass! As the name suggests, the center of mass is the point at which all surrounding mass is equally distributed. When we think of things liek gravity acting on an object, we actually calculate how it's acting on the objects center of mass. So, here’s what you need to know:

1. We can calculate the center of mass of a system algebraically or using calculus based on what we know about the object/its composition. For these calculations, see the formula sheet.
2. Always calculate the components of an object COM separately (x, y, z).
3. Sometimes we get lucky, and can use symmetry to find COM! For example, you know where the COM of a rectangle is (the midpoint vertically and horizontally).
4. For AP purposes, make sure to memorize derivations for common COM scenarios (ie rods, circles, triangles).

Energy

TLDR: the energy on the “before” side of a conservation of energy (COE) equation ALWAYS needs to be the same as the energy “after.” Did potential energy decrease? Well then, something else must have decreased.

If you don’t overthink it, consevration of energy is pretty simple, there are just a few kinds of energy we have to deal with (and the formulas are on the formula page):

The Types of Energy


1. Kinetic, the energy of movement (all moving objects have some).
2. Potential, either from gravity or something like a spring (I think of this as energy waiting to be turned kinetic, like when you almost drop a ball and it was juuust about to fall through the air if you let go).
3. What I call “loseable energy,” aka “Q,” from stuff like friction.

Solving COE Problems

I have exciting news, you now have a new option for solving a bunch of problems that previously required kinematics! This is another “need to do practice problems” situation, but basically just follow these steps:

1. Add up the energy a system had “before” and put that on the left side of an equation.
2. Add up all the energy a system has “after” and put that on the right side of the equation (so, equal to the left side).
3. Solve for missing pieces.


One last note, the “height” in your gravitational potential calculation is not absolute, you just need to make sure that if gravitational potential changes, you are being accurate about the change in “h.” So, if a ball moves from 20m in the air to 10m, you could set 20m as 10m and 10 as 0m and still get everything right.
