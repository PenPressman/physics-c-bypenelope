Up until now, all of the content on this website, and possibly all of the content you have learned if you are reading this, has focused on Newtonian mechanics, and electrostatics + magnetism. In past courses, rigid objects are enerally treated like particles when we analyze their motion. Sure, we learned how to calculate the center of mass of objects, but we don’t treat projectiles like objects made of 1015 atoms, we just treat it like one particle moving from one infinitely precise spot. Thermodynamics is totally different. If you’ve taken chemistry courses, then concepts you are familiar with will show up here quite quickly, and you’ll start to see the overlap. For this area of physics, we focus on heat and energy (you’ll see why the “and” could be replaced by a “/” soon). Thermo looks at systems and the way they change, analyzing properties and the way interactions work. If you are already taking this course and using this as a “read-some-explanations-to-review-instead-of-doing-practice-problems-because-I-am-tired” resource, skip the historical intro stuff. I’m learning this course more informally than my past physics courses, so for now it won’t be organized by unit or anything. Instead,I’ll start simple and build up. As always, use what’s useful, ignore what isn’t.

Units and Constants for ___

Remember, if there’s not much new, review what you can already do. Also, for units with multiple options, I included them in order of how often they are used in practice problems (following the standard ones).

Units

temperature (T) kelvin [K] = 1° celcius [C] - 273.15° C
pressure (P) pascal [Pa] = 9.87 atmospheres [atm] = 1 newton per square meter [N/m2]
density (ρ) kilograms per cubic meter [kg/m3]
specific heat capacity (C) joule per kilogram per kelvin (J/kg/K)
specific heat (c) calories [c]
heat (Q) joules [J]

Constants

Boltzmann's constant (KB) 1.380649 × 10−23 J/K
Stefan-Boltzmann constant (σ) 5.67 ×10−8 W⋅m−2⋅K−4
gas constant (R) 8.314 J/mol·K
avogadro's number [NA] 6.022 × 1023 mol-1
