Basic Circuits

Sticking with the format of not having diagrams for this, picture a circuit as any closed loop made of a conductive material. There will be things interrupting the wire, but we’ll get to that soon. Also, this will be a weird unit to explain solely in a conceptual way, and if you are using this page to fully learn circuits for the first time, I recommend not only drawing out diagrams as we go, but looking up more example problems and anything you find helpful. Remember that “solving a circuit” generally means analyzing all relevant variables and finding their values (current, resistance etc). You may see examples that only require you to find certain variables, but generally speaking, you’ll need to know the whole circuit to ensure you accurately answer questions about parts of it (there are a few exceptions coming soon).

Getting started, we’ll begin with the simplest possible circuits. I like to think of anything in a circuit that isn’t a wire or battery as an “interruptor,” which is, of course, not a technical term. The easiest interruptor to understand is a resistor. If the circuit is a track and the current flowing through it is a runner, resistors are hurdles slowing things down. In order to make resistance even simpler, we can combine resistors in parallel or series (see the formulas on the formula page), and analyze a mini version of the circuit before splitting things back up. And, when ready to split things back up, we can either use the tools below OR utilize the fact that current will always prioritize the path of least resistance, so it will split proportionally to the amount of resistance on any given branch of a circuit. Say we start with 10A of current and split into 2 branches: branch X has 2 ohms of resistance, and branch Y has 8. When the current splits, 8A will go to branch X, and 2A to branch Y. See what I mean by proportional?

So, the relevant things to know:
1. Voltage. Caused by an electric potential difference, you can think of voltage as the “push” moving charge through a circuit. Most importantly (other than knowing V=IR), Kirchoff’s loop rule tells us that in a closed loop, the total voltage will equal 0. This often means setting up systems of equations.

2. Current. As I mentioned above, current splits off at each branch, and the current across the whole branch will be equal throughout, regardless of resistance. Kirchhoff's node rule says that the current going into any “node” will be the same amount going out. And, keep in mind that current is a rate; specifically, the rate at which charge moves.

3. Analyzing circuits requires being very clear with your work, drawing loops when you can, and keeping track of arrows. Make sure you also keep track of the direction of current and voltage. Aside from at a battery, they will usually be opposite. As such, the direction of voltage arrows will also help you solve for voltage across multiple points. If a question asks for VAB, and the voltage arrows point from point A to point B, then VAB will be negative. Remember, this tells us the potential difference across those points. 4. Also note, we can insert ammeters or voltmeters to measure current or voltage, just remember, voltmeters must be inserted in parallel to circuits, and ammeters must be inserted in series. Simple as that.

Equivalent Resistance

Aside from memorizing the formulas for resistors in series or parallel, the reason this gets its own section is because there are a few things to keep in mind.

1. The ways to solve parallel vs series are the opposite of the ways to solve for combined spring constants, and the opposite of solving equivalent capacitance, so look things over and practice when you can to ensure you don’t mix them up.

2. Remember that to determine if resistors are in series, simply make sure that there is no way to draw a closed loop without going through both of them. If there is a way, they are not in series.

3. Add the resistors in simple series first, then parallel, then any remaining series that are clarified once things are combined.
4. If this is confusing, do some practice and hopefully it won’t be.

Capacitor Circuits

I like to think of capacitors as wires that get suuuper flattened at the end. Then, instead of touching the other wire, they simply get close enough to move the electrons on the opposite end, affecting the charge. In reality, there are lots of forms of capacitors, but we mostly see parallel plate capacitors, especially in RC circuits (coming up in a sec). The main things to know about capacitors is how they act over time, and before we get to the point where you can just memorize their behavior, understanding the foundations is helpful. When t=0, and the circuit first starts up, the capacitor has no charge stored in it, so it is able to act like a closed switch, and just continue the behavior of the wire. I haven't spent time talking about switches because to be honest, I don’t want to overcomplicate it, but I think you can infer: when switches are open, the loop isn’t closed and current cannot flow. As such, when t=0, there is momentarily normal flow. On the flip side, when t=∞ (or really just when enough time has passed to fill the capacitor up), the capacitor is fully charged up, and the lack of potential difference cuts off the flow of charge, making it an open switch. For the more complex stuff, here’s everything else you need to know:


1. One way to influence capacitance is to add a “dielectric” inside the capacitor. This acts as an electric insulator that has us add a constant, depending on the material, to the formula for capacitance. In fact, we can always add a dielectric constant to a formula whenever we have previously assumed there was a vacuum (which you can tell by the presence of the permittivity of free space constant (ε0) in a formula).

2. Aside from using the formulas for time in between 0 and ∞, just picture a capacitor “filling up” and pushing electrons along across the gap. Additionally, remember the special time “tau,” equal to RC, at which time we’ll have 63.2% of our total voltage.

3. I’ve included the formulas for a few other specific capacitors, but practice deriving them by starting with Gauss’ law to find E, then finding the potential difference, and then plugging in to Q=CV to find C. Don’t worry, Q will cancel.

Resistance Capacitor (“RC”) Circuits

If everything up to this point has made sense, then there isn’t anything you technically need to add to solve RC circuits. However, they’ll obviously get more complicated, so it's extra important to write down everything you can and use equivalent resistance and capacitance to simplify things as much as you can and work from there. Additionally, any time you have an open switch or capacitor at t=0, remember to think of any loop that is now open as a “turned off” branch of the circuit with no current flowing. You should be excited to see this show up, it makes things easier! Now, PRACTICE, PRACTICE, PRACTICE! Good luck :)

Other

I’m going to format this part in a list, because it’s all kind of random.


1. Batteries: This just takes resistance circuits a step further. Now, instead of just assuming the overall voltage of the battery, analyze the circuit as usual to find the internal resistance, and solve for overall voltage (aka “terminal voltage”) of the battery by subtracting internal R x I from the initial voltage (aka “EMF”).

2. Wires: Until now, we’ve been simplifying them, pretending they are basically exempt from any real-world properties that they obviously do have, i.e. resistance, volume, etc. So, use the formula to factor their resistance in if you need. Though, for AP purposes, don’t worry, they won’t be included in more complex circuit problems.

If you want to take a look at the extra formulas I included, they’ll give you insight into other things we now have a foundation for, like charge density, resistivity, and conductivity. Hopefully it makes sense that conductivity is the inverse of resistivity!

SI Units for Circuits


current (I) amps [A]
voltage (v) volts [v] (volts = joules per coulomb, j/c)
resistance (R) ohms [Ω]
capacitance (C) farads [f] (farads = coulombs per volt, c/v)
energy density (μ) joule per cubic meter [J/m2]
resistivity (ρ) ohm meters [Ωm]
tau (τ) seconds [s]
